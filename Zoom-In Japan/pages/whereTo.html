SHRINES

SHINTO SHRINES are places of worshipping the kami, the Shinto "gods". People visit shrines in order to pay respect to the kami or to pray for good fortune. Shrines are also visited during special events such as New Year and other festivals. New born babies are traditionally brought to a shrine a few weeks after birth, and many couples hold their wedding ceremonies there.

TEMPLES

TEMPLES are the place of workshipping Buddism. Buddism was imported to Japan from China and Korea in the 6th century. Today, roughly two thirds of the Japanese population consider themselves Buddhist. Funerals are usually carried out in a Buddhist way and are places to show respect for ancestors, while shrines are places for wedding and celebrations.

CASTLES

CASTLES arose in the 15th century after the central government's authority had weakened and Japan had fallen into the chaotic era of warring states (sengoku jidai). After the end of the feudal age (1868), many castles were destroyed as unwelcom relics of the past or were lost in World War II. Only dozen "original castles" with a main keep that dates from feudal era (before 1868), survive today.

Source from japan-guide.com

Original Castle

HIMEJI Castle aka. White Heron Castle (Shirasagijo) due to its white appearance. It is considered as Japan's most spectacular castle for its impressive size and well preserved beauty. Unlike many othe Japanese castles, it was never destroyed by war, earthquake or fire and survives to this day as one of the country's twelve original castles.

MATSUYAMA Castle aka. Bitchu-Matsuyama Castle to differentiate from Matsuyama Castle in Shikoku, stands on a steep mountain north of Takahashi's town center. It is the oldest surviving castle in Japan and the only mountaintop castle among the twelve orginal castles which have survived the post- feudal age intact. At an elevation of 430 meters, it is also Japan's highest catsle.

Reconstructed Site

SHURI Castle served as the administrative center and residence of the Ryukyo kings for several centuries until Okinawa became a Japanese prefecture in 1879. KOCHI Castle

Original Castle

KOCHI Castle was first constructed between 1601 and 1622, but most of its main buildings date from 1748 when they were reconstructed after a fire. It used to be the seat of Yamauchi lords, who ruled over the surrounding Tosharegion, during the Edo Period.

Click to Enlarge

ODAWARA Castle

Reconstructed Site

ODAWARA Castle was originally built in the mid 15th century and soon fell into the hands of the Hojo Clan, one of the most powerful clans during the Warring States Era. In 1703, the castle was destroyed by an earthquake but quickly rebuilt; yet in 1870, its keep and other buildings were dismantled and sold. The keep was rebuilt again in 1960 based on models and drawings from the Edo Period. Over the following years, the three major gates leading up to the castle were also restored. Today it is the closest castle keep to Tokyo.

HIROSAKI Castle

Original Castle

It was built in 1611 by the Tsugaru Clan. The castle is located in Hirosaki Park, a spacious public park of about 0.6 square kilometers. The original five-story keep burnt down in 1627 after being struck by lightning and rebuilt in 1810.
